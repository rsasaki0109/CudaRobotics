\begin{abstract}
Sampling-based model predictive controllers such as MPPI handle nonlinear
dynamics and nonconvex costs effectively, but their trajectory quality can
plateau under limited rollout budgets on hard tasks.  Recent work shows that
MPPI is equivalent to a preconditioned gradient descent step.  We study a
minimal hybrid controller that adds a short autodiff refinement---just three
local gradient steps---to the standard MPPI update.  With parallelized
gradient computation on GPU, this refinement preserves most of the sampling
budget within the same wall-clock time.  We evaluate the method against
vanilla MPPI and eight strong non-hybrid baselines using fixed-budget sweeps
and exact matched-time comparisons on two-dimensional dynamic-obstacle
navigation and a 7-DOF serial-arm manipulation benchmark.  On the hardest
dynamic-obstacle task, the hybrid controller is the only method that reaches
the goal across all tested compute budgets from 0.2 to 3.1\,ms---all eight
non-hybrid baselines remain unsuccessful across every fixed-$K$ setting and
in the matched-time sweeps we evaluated.  On 7-DOF manipulation with dynamic
obstacles, the hybrid controller achieves success\,=\,1.00 at 0.84\,ms while
the closest feedback baseline reaches 0.75 at 4.01\,ms.  A gradient
freshness analysis shows that the autodiff correction concentrates during
obstacle encounters where the cost landscape changes most rapidly,
explaining why the refinement is specifically valuable on dynamic tasks.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Motion planning, model predictive control, sampling-based optimization,
gradient-based refinement, GPU-accelerated control

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) control is widely used for real-time
nonlinear trajectory optimization because it handles arbitrary cost functions
through parallel rollout sampling on GPU~\cite{williams2017mppi}.  However,
under limited rollout budgets, vanilla MPPI can produce trajectories that are
qualitatively close to the solution without crossing the success
boundary---particularly on tasks with rapidly changing cost landscapes such
as dynamic obstacle avoidance.

A growing body of work augments MPPI with various refinement mechanisms:
gradient-based DDP smoothing~\cite{mppi_ipddp}, learned sampling
distributions~\cite{le2026step_mppi,step_mppi}, sensitivity-derived feedback
gains~\cite{belvedere2026feedback_mppi}, and ancillary controller
biasing~\cite{trevisan2024biased_mppi}.  Recent theoretical work by Fazlyab
et al.~\cite{fazlyab2026mppi_gd} shows that classical MPPI is exactly a
preconditioned gradient descent step with unit step size on a KL-regularized
distribution objective, establishing a principled connection between
sampling-based and gradient-based trajectory optimization.

In this context, we study a deliberately minimal hybrid controller: after the
standard MPPI weighted update, we apply three local gradient steps to the
nominal control sequence using forward-mode autodiff.  The key design choice
is simplicity---no learned components, no convex corridor construction, no
additional data structures beyond the cost gradient itself.

Our main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A minimal, training-free hybrid MPPI controller where the autodiff
    refinement is parallelized to preserve most of the sampling budget at
    matched wall-clock time, interpretable as adding explicit gradient steps
    after MPPI's implicit preconditioned gradient step.
  \item A matched-time evaluation and robustness sweep showing a non-hybrid
    Pareto ceiling: on the hardest dynamic-obstacle task, all eight
    non-hybrid baselines remain unsuccessful across every fixed-$K$ setting
    and every matched-time configuration we tested,
    while the hybrid controller succeeds at $K\!=\!128$ and above.
  \item Evidence across 2D dynamic navigation and 7-DOF serial-arm
    manipulation, including a gradient freshness analysis showing that the
    autodiff correction concentrates during dynamic obstacle encounters where
    the sampling-based preconditioner is least effective.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{MPPI and path-integral control.}
MPPI~\cite{williams2017mppi} performs weighted averaging of sampled
trajectories using the exponential cost-to-go as importance weights.  Fazlyab
et al.~\cite{fazlyab2026mppi_gd} show this update is equivalent to a
preconditioned gradient descent step, connecting MPPI to optimization-based
MPC.

\textbf{Sampling + gradient hybrids.}
CEM-GD~\cite{bharadhwaj2020cemgd} combines cross-entropy method sampling
with gradient descent for model-based RL planning.
MPPI-IPDDP~\cite{mppi_ipddp} smooths MPPI trajectories via DDP inside a
convex corridor.  Our approach is simpler: pure autodiff on the post-MPPI
control sequence without corridor construction or learning.

\textbf{Feedback-enhanced MPPI.}
Feedback-MPPI~\cite{belvedere2026feedback_mppi} computes local feedback gains
from rollout sensitivity analysis.
Biased-MPPI~\cite{trevisan2024biased_mppi} uses ancillary controllers to
improve sampling efficiency.
Step-MPPI~\cite{step_mppi} learns a neural sampling distribution for
single-step lookahead.  Our method is complementary: training-free refinement
applied post-MPPI rather than modifying the sampling distribution.

\textbf{GPU-accelerated control.}
DiffMPC~\cite{diffmpc2025} provides GPU-accelerated differentiable MPC in
JAX for learning applications.  cuNRTO~\cite{cunrto2026} solves robust
trajectory optimization on GPU for manipulators.
MPPI-Generic~\cite{mppi_generic2024} provides a CUDA MPPI library.  We share
the GPU-parallel rollout design but focus on the gradient refinement
analysis.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Controller Structure}
\label{sec:controller_structure}

At each control step, the hybrid controller executes three phases, summarized
in Algorithm~\ref{alg:diff_mppi}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Diff-MPPI Controller (one control step)}
\label{alg:diff_mppi}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Current state $\mathbf{x}_0$, nominal controls $\mathbf{u}_{0:T-1}$, sample count $K$, gradient steps $N_g$, step size $\alpha$, clip threshold $c$
\ENSURE Updated nominal controls $\mathbf{u}_{0:T-1}$, applied control $\mathbf{u}_0$
\STATE \textbf{--- Phase 1: MPPI Sampling Update ---}
\STATE Sample $K$ perturbations $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}^{(k)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)$
\STATE Roll out $K$ trajectories: $\tilde{\mathbf{u}}^{(k)} = \mathbf{u} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}^{(k)}$
\STATE Compute trajectory costs $S^{(k)}$ \COMMENT{$K$ GPU threads}
\STATE Compute weights $w^{(k)} \propto \exp(-\frac{1}{\lambda} S^{(k)})$
\STATE $\mathbf{u} \leftarrow \mathbf{u} + \sum_k w^{(k)} \boldsymbol{\epsilon}^{(k)}$
\STATE \textbf{--- Phase 2: Parallel Cost Gradient ---}
\STATE Roll out nominal trajectory $\mathbf{x}_{0:T}$ under $\mathbf{u}_{0:T-1}$
\STATE Compute stage cost gradients $\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \ell_t$, $\nabla_{\mathbf{u}} \ell_t$ \COMMENT{$T$ GPU threads}
\STATE Compute dynamics Jacobians $A_t$, $B_t$ \COMMENT{$T$ GPU threads}
\STATE \textbf{--- Phase 3: Local Gradient Refinement ---}
\FOR{$i = 1$ \TO $N_g$}
  \STATE Backward adjoint pass: $\boldsymbol{\lambda}_T = \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \ell_T$
  \FOR{$t = T\!-\!1$ \TO $0$}
    \STATE $\boldsymbol{\lambda}_t = \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \ell_t + A_t^\top \boldsymbol{\lambda}_{t+1}$
    \STATE $\mathbf{g}_t = \nabla_{\mathbf{u}} \ell_t + B_t^\top \boldsymbol{\lambda}_{t+1}$
  \ENDFOR
  \STATE Clip: $\mathbf{g}_t \leftarrow \mathbf{g}_t \cdot \min(1,\; c / \|\mathbf{g}_t\|)$ for all $t$
  \STATE $\mathbf{u}_t \leftarrow \mathbf{u}_t - \alpha\, \mathbf{g}_t$ for all $t$
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $\mathbf{u}_{0:T-1}$, apply $\mathbf{u}_0$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textbf{Phase~1: MPPI Sampling Update.}
Sample $K$ rollouts around the current nominal control sequence~$\mathbf{u}$.
Compute trajectory costs and update the nominal via the standard MPPI
weighted average.

\textbf{Phase~2: Parallel Cost Gradient Computation.}
Roll out the updated nominal trajectory.  Compute stage cost gradients and
dynamics Jacobians in parallel across $T$ horizon threads on GPU.

\textbf{Phase~3: Local Gradient Refinement.}
Apply the adjoint backward pass (single-thread, matrix operations only) to
obtain control gradients.  Take three gradient descent steps on the control
sequence with per-timestep norm clipping.

The gradient computation is split into a parallel phase ($T$ threads compute
cost gradients and analytical dynamics Jacobians simultaneously) and a
sequential phase (backward adjoint accumulates control gradients via matrix
multiplication).  This separation enables parallelization of the expensive
cost gradient evaluations while preserving the sequential adjoint dependency.

\subsection{Compute Budget Analysis}
\label{sec:budget}

After parallelization, the gradient refinement adds minimal overhead to the
MPPI sampling step.  At the exact 1.0\,ms target on
\texttt{dynamic\_slalom}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Vanilla MPPI: $K = 7167$ samples
  \item Diff-MPPI-3: $K = 5966$ samples + 3 gradient steps
\end{itemize}

The hybrid controller retains 83\% of the pure-sampling budget while gaining
gradient refinement within the same wall-clock regime.  This makes the
compute--quality comparison genuinely fair: the refinement is not buying
quality by escaping the timing budget.

\subsection{Adaptive Gradient Skip}
\label{sec:adaptive}

We additionally implement an adaptive variant that monitors the total gradient
norm and skips the gradient step when the norm falls below a threshold.  On
2D navigation tasks, the gradient computation is already fast enough that the
monitoring overhead exceeds the savings.  The adaptive skip is more valuable
for higher-dimensional systems where gradient steps are more expensive.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{2D Dynamic-Obstacle Navigation}

Two scenarios on a bicycle model: \texttt{dynamic\_crossing} (easier, one
crossing obstacle) and \texttt{dynamic\_slalom} (harder, multiple dynamic
obstacles requiring precise timing).  Goal tolerance 2.0\,m, max 260~steps.

\subsection{7-DOF Serial-Arm Manipulation}

Panda-like 7-DOF arm with 14D state (7 joint angles + 7 velocities), 7D
torque control, 3D workspace obstacles, second-order joint dynamics with
gravity and damping, and analytical dynamics Jacobians.  Two scenarios:
\texttt{7dof\_shelf\_reach} (static obstacle) and
\texttt{7dof\_dynamic\_avoid} (moving 3D obstacle).

\subsection{Baselines}

Eight non-hybrid baselines are included in the benchmark package.  The
strongest and closest ones are highlighted in the main exact-time table;
broader family variants appear in the matched-time robustness sweeps:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{mppi}: vanilla sampling-only MPPI.
  \item \textbf{feedback\_mppi\_ref}: released current-action
    Feedback-MPPI-style gains~\cite{belvedere2026feedback_mppi}.
  \item \textbf{feedback\_mppi\_fused}: covariance + LQR blend
    (strongest non-hybrid).
  \item \textbf{feedback\_mppi\_hf}: two-rate controller with full-horizon
    covariance/LQR gains.
  \item \textbf{feedback\_mppi\_faithful}: two-rate controller with
    current-action-only gains.
  \item \textbf{feedback\_mppi\_cov}: covariance-regression gains.
  \item \textbf{feedback\_mppi\_paper}: every-step covariance + LQR blend,
    closest to the strongest principled
    Feedback-MPPI-style baseline~\cite{belvedere2026feedback_mppi}.
  \item \textbf{step\_mppi}: online sampling-bias EMA inspired by
    Step-MPPI~\cite{step_mppi}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Matched-Time Evaluation Protocol}

We use a binary-search exact-time tuning script that finds the $K$ value per
planner and scenario that produces a given wall-clock target (e.g.,
1.0\,ms).  This ensures all comparisons are at the same per-step compute
budget, not just the same~$K$.  The main text reports a fixed-controller
exact-time table for the submission-critical baselines, while broader
family-level multi-parameter sweeps over feedback gain scale and Diff-MPPI
gradient hyperparameters are treated as robustness follow-up.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Non-Hybrid Pareto Ceiling on \texttt{dynamic\_slalom}}

The central result: on \texttt{dynamic\_slalom}, \textbf{all eight
non-hybrid baselines remain unsuccessful across every fixed-$K$ sweep and
exact-time run we tested}.  The strongest non-hybrid baseline
(\texttt{feedback\_mppi\_fused}) reduces final distance to about 10.2--10.3,
but never reaches the goal.

\texttt{diff\_mppi\_3} succeeds at every $K$ from 128 upward, reaching final
distance 1.89--1.95.

Table~\ref{tab:slalom} shows a compact exact-time 1.0\,ms comparison for the
strongest and most submission-critical baselines; the remaining feedback
variants follow the same qualitative failure pattern and are omitted for
space.

Notably, the \texttt{step\_mppi} baseline---which adapts its sampling
distribution online via cost-weighted exponential moving average---performs at
vanilla MPPI level (final distance 14.20 at $K\!=\!1024$), indicating that
learning a better sampling distribution alone does not resolve the dynamic
obstacle challenge.

This establishes a hard non-hybrid Pareto ceiling: no amount of compute
budget allocated to non-hybrid controllers can solve this task.  The gradient
refinement is the only mechanism that crosses the success boundary.

A broader family-level matched-time sweep over $K$, feedback gain scale, and
Diff-MPPI gradient hyperparameters reaches the same qualitative conclusion at
1.0, 1.5, and 2.0\,ms: all non-hybrid families still fail on
\texttt{dynamic\_slalom}.  The best hybrid family point shifts between
\texttt{diff\_mppi\_3} and \texttt{diff\_mppi\_1} depending on the target, so
we treat that sweep as robustness evidence rather than the headline table.

\subsection{Gradient Freshness Analysis}

Comparing gradient behavior on static (\texttt{corner\_turn}) vs.\ dynamic
(\texttt{dynamic\_slalom}) tasks reveals:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Gradient magnitude} peaks during obstacle encounters on
    dynamic tasks, while declining monotonically on static tasks.
  \item \textbf{Correction magnitude} concentrates in the episode segment
    where dynamic obstacles are closest.
  \item \textbf{Gradient-correction alignment} varies more on dynamic tasks,
    indicating the gradient provides directional information not captured by
    the MPPI update.
\end{itemize}

This supports the interpretation that the gradient refinement acts as a
``freshness correction'': on dynamic tasks where the MPPI warm-start becomes
stale due to obstacle motion, the gradient provides an update based on the
current cost landscape.

\end{figure}

\subsection{Feedback Architecture Analysis}

The \texttt{feedback\_mppi\_faithful} variant tests whether a two-rate
controller architecture (replan every other step, apply stored current-action
gains between replans) can replicate the benefit of gradient refinement.
Result: it fails on both dynamic tasks even at $K\!=\!8192$ (2.1\,ms),
performing at vanilla MPPI level on \texttt{dynamic\_slalom}.  Combined with
the \texttt{step\_mppi} result showing that an improved sampling distribution
also fails, this confirms that the gradient refinement provides complementary
value that neither pure feedback nor learned sampling can replicate.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The current contribution is empirical with theoretical motivation from
Fazlyab et al.~\cite{fazlyab2026mppi_gd}.  We now include
\texttt{feedback\_mppi\_paper} and \texttt{step\_mppi}, but both remain
in-harness reimplementations rather than full reproductions of external
controller stacks, and neither closes the \texttt{dynamic\_slalom} gap.  The
7-DOF benchmark uses a simplified dynamics model rather than a validated
Panda simulation.  We now include small MuJoCo
\texttt{InvertedPendulum-v4} and \texttt{Reacher} follow-ups, but those read
primarily as transfer / standardization checks rather than decisive
hybrid-only wins, so the broader evaluation still relies mostly on custom
benchmark domains rather than standardized manipulation or locomotion suites.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We showed that a minimal gradient refinement---three autodiff steps after a
standard MPPI update---breaks through a hard non-hybrid Pareto ceiling on
dynamic-obstacle tasks.  With parallelized gradient computation, the
refinement preserves most of the sampling budget at matched wall-clock time.
Neither \texttt{feedback\_mppi\_paper} nor \texttt{step\_mppi} closes the
\texttt{dynamic\_slalom} gap.  A gradient freshness analysis explains why the
refinement is specifically valuable on dynamic tasks: the gradient provides
current-landscape information that the sampling-based update cannot capture
when obstacles move between timesteps.  The approach transfers to 7-DOF
manipulation, where the hybrid controller achieves higher success at lower
compute than the closest feedback baseline.
